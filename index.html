CASE STUDIES

Each project below is presented as a consulting story. The goal is to make the reasoning visible: what was broken, how the analysis was framed, what was redesigned, and what changed before versus after.

Case Study 01 · Operations

Multi-country service performance cockpit

Outcome: faster operational review

The problem

Operational teams were reviewing performance through fragmented files, manual exports, and disconnected service indicators. Leaders could see daily numbers, but not the full relationship between offered volume, handled calls, SLA, forecast accuracy, and service quality.

What I analyzed

I mapped the KPI chain from incoming demand to handled output, identified duplicated calculations, and separated descriptive indicators from decision indicators. The objective was to remove noise and make exception management more visible.

What I defined

A structured dashboard layout with filter discipline, a central KPI table, side metric cards, and a cleaner metric hierarchy so supervisors and managers could use the same source of truth during daily reviews.

The solution

Designed an operations cockpit combining volume, SLA, pick-up, AHT, forecast accuracy, short calls, and waiting time in one clean visual logic. The dashboard moved from raw reporting to guided performance management.

Before
  • Manual consolidation from multiple views
  • Inconsistent KPI reading by stakeholders
  • Slow escalation during service deterioration
After
  • Single daily control layer for operational review
  • Clear metric ownership and faster anomaly spotting
  • More structured manager conversations
Case Study 02 · Executive reporting

Executive KPI monitoring and trend simplification

Outcome: better decision readability

The problem

Leadership dashboards contained the right numbers but not the right reading path. Trend cards, monthly charts, and comparative metrics were not arranged to support quick interpretation during management reviews.

What I analyzed

I reviewed how stakeholders consumed information in meetings, which KPIs triggered decisions, and where the visual hierarchy broke down. The issue was less about data availability and more about executive usability.

What I defined

A top-down layout with compact KPI cards, trend panels, service bars, and a handled-vs-offered section to create a cleaner visual narrative from summary to diagnosis.

The solution

Rebuilt the reporting page into an executive overview dashboard using consistent spacing, aligned chart titles, and comparative PY/CY framing so each panel answered a specific management question.

Before
  • High density without clear reading order
  • Too much effort to extract key messages
  • Monthly reviews slowed by visual confusion
After
  • Sharper executive summary at first glance
  • Faster move from overview to root-cause conversation
  • More confidence in KPI interpretation
Case Study 03 · Banking

Retail banking service operations control tower

Outcome: backlog and response control

The problem

A banking support environment was managing queues with limited visibility across backlog, response time, aging buckets, and automation contribution. Service managers could react to volume spikes, but not easily prioritize what required action first.

What I analyzed

I broke the workflow into ticket intake, aging progression, throughput, and SLA risk. The analysis focused on where customer experience deteriorated: old queues, slow response time, and workload imbalance between manual and automated streams.

What I defined

A control-tower dashboard with productivity, backlog, response-time KPIs, a queue aging distribution, automation rate, and breach-driver analysis. The structure was designed to support operations managers, not only reporting teams.

The solution

Delivered a banking operations panel that translated queue data into a prioritization story: where work was accumulating, how quickly teams were responding, and how automation could relieve manual pressure.

Before
  • Backlog viewed as one total, not a risk profile
  • Response-time trends hard to connect to queue aging
  • Automation contribution not visible enough
After
  • Backlog segmented by urgency and aging logic
  • Managers can identify where delays start building
  • Automation is positioned as an operational lever
Case Study 04 · Workforce

Workforce coverage, shrinkage, and adherence planning panel

Outcome: clearer planning discipline

The problem

Workforce planning data existed, but leaders lacked a single view linking staffing requirement, coverage, adherence, shrinkage, and intraday capacity. Planning discussions were often reactive instead of controlled.

What I analyzed

I identified the variables most relevant to decision-making: demand versus staffing, schedule reality versus plan, and the operational effect of shrinkage. The aim was to convert workforce numbers into management signals.

What I defined

A workforce control panel combining plan-versus-requirement trends, occupancy and utilization cards, intraday capacity heatmap, and shrinkage mix to support daily and weekly staffing decisions.

The solution

Created a decision-oriented dashboard that aligned planning metrics with real operational questions: do we have enough staff, where do we lose productive capacity, and how stable is schedule adherence?

Before
  • Planning variables reviewed separately
  • Too much manual interpretation in meetings
  • Shrinkage impact not clearly visualized
After
  • Unified workforce decision view
  • Better planning conversations between teams
  • Faster visibility on staffing risk and adherence
